THE SKY'S THE LIMIT

  • Epic
• 2017
8.7

Best new music

  • by Jayson Greene
    Contributing Editor
  • Rap
  • Experimental
April 1 2017

Featured Tracks:

"Hacker"

—

TRAP LORDE$

Via SoundCloud

TRAP LORDE$are angry. It's unclear why. But their thirst for vengeance, their monomaniacal desire to visit fiery destruction on the powers-that-be, is crystal-clear on THE SKY'S THE LIMIT, even if nothing else-- where the hell this album came from; who plays which instrument; what the lead singer is yelling about; and what on earth this band of insurgents is doing-- makes much sense at all. When playing this album, the only thing I'm sure of is my overwhelming desire to split my forehead open on a cinder block.

The Bellingham group seems to have landed from an alternate planet, or at least an alternate decade when defiantly mutant outfits like this were occasionally given major-label backing. They've been persistently tagged as "joke rap" for context, but it's not a very useful description of their music. For starters, not much of THE SKY'S THE LIMIT scans as a joke: It's confrontational, abrasive, and chaotic. Most of this album is an alien swarm of buzzing and sputtering noises. 

THE SKY'S THE LIMIT is a million-mph blur of ideas. One can only imagine how many hours it took to make Michael Remington's production sound like it's traveling inward from every corner of the mix toward its center, but the music seems to be constantly lunging out at you from all sides. A Bollywood vocal sample is obliterated by a mortar-round hailstorm of viciously treated percussion. A tar-thick drone of indeterminate origin (guitar? computer?) pops into a glitter of synthesized voices. And on the final track, the song hits a peak that the entire album seems to gather towards: it's the only song TRAP LORDE$ have recorded so far that tugs at your hips as much as it bludgeons your skull.

As for "rap": To call what lead vocalist Matt Woodruff (aka Bubba Patron) does "rapping" stretches the definition of the word beyond what even an avowed Lil B and Waka Flocka Flame fan like me can endorse. Woodruff's deranged shouting brings a lot of things to mind-- Mark E. Smith with his mouth full, Jim Jones during an air raid, Sloth from The Goonies-- but rapping isn't one of them. Follow his lines closely and you'll slam up against the realization that you're largely transcribing word salad. But his hoarse, panicked voice functions as primal fight-or-fight communication: Things Are Not All Right.

The clearest link through all the pop-culture static to the music TRAP LORDE$ make is back to the ultra-aggressive, defiantly ignorant, and proudly dumb American hardcore rap moment of the 1980s. I don't watch a lot of skate videos these days, but I know a great highlight-reel song when I hear it, and every moment of THE SKY'S THE LIMIT qualifies. TRAP LORDE$ appeals to the knuckle-dragging troglodyte and the smirking smart kid in us: thick-headed goonery and bookish, viscera-free nerdiness, making beautifully misanthropic music together. Granted, THE SKY'S THE LIMIT is about as intellectual an experience as a scraped knee. But it's just as good at reminding you that you're alive.
