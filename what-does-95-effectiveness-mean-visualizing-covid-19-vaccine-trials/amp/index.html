What Does 95% Effectiveness Mean? Visualizing Covid-19 Vaccine Trials

Michael Remington - 25 Mar 2021

You've heard the numbers: Pfizer-BioNTech is 95% effective at preventing Covid-19. But what does that mean? Do you have a 5% chance of getting Covid-19? Thankfully, the answer is no.

For this post I’ll examine Pfizer-BioNTech data, but the same logic applies to Moderna and other trials. The 95% number comes from table 9 of the Pfizer-BioNTech briefing document available at fda.gov.

In the placebo group, 162 people tested positive for Covid-19, 17,349 people did not. In the vaccinated group, 8 people tested positive for Covid-19, 17,403 people did not. Visualizing these numbers gives a clearer picture:

Put another way, 0.925% of the placebo group and 0.046% of the vaccinated group tested positive:

So where does 95% come from? The “Vaccinated” bar above is about 5% of the length of the “Placebo” bar, and 100-5 = 95%.

95% efficacy doesn’t mean 5% chance of failure, but that there was a 95% improvement compared to the unvaccinated group. This is called relative risk reduction.

If not 5%, then what was the chance of getting Covid-19 in the vaccinated group? 8 out of 17,411 tested positive in the vaccinated group, or 0.046% over the median follow-up length of 2 months. This percentage may change over a longer follow-up time. We'll know more once phase 3 trials are complete.

An article in The Lancet medical journal gives the same numbers, but rounded:

with a cumulated COVID-19 attack rate over a period of 3 months of about 1% without a vaccine, we would expect roughly 0.05% of vaccinated people would get diseased. It does not mean that 95% of people are protected from disease with the vaccine—a general misconception of vaccine protection

To summarize: Over the duration of this trial, the Pfizer-BioNTech vaccine reduced the absolute risk of getting Covid-19 from 0.925% to 0.046%, representing a 95% reduction in relative risk.

Do these trials apply to the real world?

The absolute probabilities only apply to the duration of the trial. More than 1% of the placebo group would've been infected if the trial had monitored them for longer. 28% of the US is estimated to have been infected as of March 7, 2021. Here is a chart from the FDA document that shows how cases rose over time in the trial:

These trends likely continued after the trial. That's why it's the relative effectiveness, 95%, that's publicized.

Another thing to note, all vaccines were 100% effective at preventing hospitalization and death from Covid-19.
